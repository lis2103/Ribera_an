Predictions and Insights for the Elderly Population

  • What social and health services will the elderly population require in the coming years, from 2025 to 2030? Based on our research and predictive models, we foresee an increase in the need for social and healthcare services for the elderly population between 2025 and 2030. Hospitalization rates for people over 60 are projected to rise from 14% to 15%, readmissions after 30 days from 11.5% to 13%, and emergency incidents from 125 to 135 per 1000 individuals aged 60+. The growing prevalence of sedentary lifestyles and obesity in the western world is contributing to chronic diseases, leading to increased healthcare demands. Hospitals should prepare by expanding capacity and enhancing emergency services to meet these projected needs
  • Are there differences according to the regions of Spain and urban/rural areas? Our research and predictive models highlight significant disparities in healthcare needs and service availability between urban and rural areas in Spain. Urban areas will likely see a higher demand for health tech solutions and services as elderly populations move to cities for better access to healthcare and social services. In contrast, rural areas face challenges like 'medical deserts,' higher obesity rates, and limited access to specialized care. These regions also show a slower adoption of technology. The priority in rural areas should be improving general healthcare access, possibly through teleconsultations and medicine delivery systems. This rural-urban disparity necessitates tailored healthcare services to ensure equitable access across regions
  • And according to the profile of the elderly (archetypes)? Based on our research and predictive models, distinct profiles within the elderly population emerge, particularly when considering socioeconomic status (SES) and health behaviors. High SES individuals generally have better health behaviors, are less likely to be obese, and seek more specialized care with higher expectations for quality and accessibility. They are particularly interested in personalized care facilitated by health tech. Conversely, lower SES individuals face higher rates of chronic conditions and greater disparities in healthcare access. Their focus is more on the accessibility and affordability of basic services. Customizing service delivery to cater to these profiles—such as focusing on preventive care and health tech for high SES individuals and improving basic healthcare access for lower SES groups—can enhance patient satisfaction and outcomes.
  • What role will digital technology play? Our research and predictive models indicate a growing trend in the use of technology for personal health management. Words like 'tech' and 'science' are predicted to grow in use, showing a rising enthusiasm for incorporating technology into healthcare. With increasing smartphone ownership, wearable device usage, and digital health app adoption, telemedicine, remote monitoring, and digital health management are poised to play significant roles in elderly care by 2025-2030. These technologies can reduce hospital visits, allow for timely interventions, and facilitate health management through apps. Urban areas, with better access to technology and higher SES individuals, will likely benefit more immediately from these innovations. However, addressing the technological divide in rural areas is crucial to ensure broader adoption and effectiveness. This includes improving basic healthcare access and providing the necessary infrastructure and support for digital solutions.
